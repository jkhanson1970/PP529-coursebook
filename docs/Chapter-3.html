Chapter 3: Descriptive Statistics

Once we have collected data, the first task is to describe what we have. Before we test hypotheses, estimate models, or make claims about relationships between variables, we need to understand the basic shape and structure of the data.

This is the purpose of descriptive statistics.

Descriptive statistics summarize data. They help us answer basic but essential questions:

  • What values appear in the data?
  • Which values are most common?
  • What is typical?
  • How spread out are the data?
  • Are there extreme values?
  • Is the distribution symmetric, or is it skewed?
  • Which summary statistics are appropriate for this variable?

This chapter introduces the core tools of descriptive statistics. We begin with distributions, which show how values of a variable appear across a sample. We then discuss measures of central tendency, including the mean, median, and mode. After that, we turn to measures of dispersion, including the range, interquartile range, variance, and standard deviation. Finally, we consider skewness, which describes asymmetry in a distribution.

Throughout the chapter, one principle remains central: the appropriate descriptive statistic depends on the measurement level of the variable. Nominal, ordinal, and interval-level variables cannot always be summarized in the same ways.

1. Distributions

A distribution describes how the values of a variable appear in a sample or population.

More specifically, a distribution tells us the relative frequency with which different values of a variable occur. Some values may appear often, while others may appear rarely. Describing that pattern is one of the first steps in understanding data.

For example, suppose we have survey data on party identification. We may want to know how many respondents identify as Democrats, Republicans, Independents, or members of another party. The distribution of party identification tells us how the sample is divided across those categories.

Or suppose we have data on age. Some ages may be more common than others. A sample of undergraduate students will likely have many people between 18 and 22, while a sample of registered voters will likely have a much wider age distribution.

Distributions can be described in tables, graphs, and summary statistics.

Two broad features of distributions are especially important:

  1. Central tendency
    Where is the “typical” value?

  2. Dispersion
    How spread out are the values?

Before we can summarize a distribution, however, we need to think about the type of variable we are describing.

2. Distributions of Categorical and Discrete Variables

For categorical or discrete variables, we often begin with a frequency distribution.

A frequency distribution shows how many cases fall into each value or category of a variable.

For example, consider a survey question about religious service attendance. Respondents may be categorized as follows:

Religious Service Attendance Frequency Percent Cumulative Percent
Never 2,377 40.4% 40.4%
A few times a year 880 15.0% 55.4%
Once or twice a month 749 12.7% 68.1%
Almost every week 559 9.5% 77.6%
Every week 964 16.4% 94.0%
More than once a week 353 6.0% 100.0%
Total 5,882 100.0%

The frequency column gives the number of respondents in each category.

The percent column gives the relative frequency of each category. For example, 40.4 percent of respondents said they never attend religious services.

The cumulative percent column adds categories as we move down the table. After the first row, we have accounted for 40.4 percent of the sample. After the second row, we have accounted for 55.4 percent. By the final row, we have accounted for 100 percent of the sample.

Frequency tables are especially useful for categorical variables because they show which categories are common and which are rare.

The same information can also be displayed visually using a bar chart.

A bar chart is appropriate for categorical or discrete data because the values are distinct from one another. The bars should be separated to indicate that the categories are separate.

For example, a bar chart of religious service attendance would have one bar for “Never,” another for “A few times a year,” another for “Once or twice a month,” and so on. The height of each bar could represent either the frequency or the percentage of respondents in that category.

The tallest bar identifies the most common category. In this example, “Never” is the most common response.

3. Distributions of Continuous Variables

For continuous variables, the logic is slightly different.

A continuous variable can, in principle, take on infinitely many values within a range. Examples include:

  • age
  • income
  • height
  • weight
  • temperature
  • time
  • distance

Because continuous variables can take so many possible values, we usually do not list every individual value in a frequency table. Instead, we group values into ranges called bins.

For example, if we are describing age in a survey of adults, we might use bins such as:

Age Range Frequency Percent Cumulative Percent
18–29 451 21.5% 21.5%
30–39 386 18.4% 39.9%
40–49 359 17.1% 57.0%
50–59 402 19.2% 76.2%
60–69 301 14.4% 90.6%
70+ 197 9.4% 100.0%

The exact bins depend on the data and the purpose of the analysis. Often, bins are equal in width. For age, we might use ten-year intervals. But sometimes we use wider bins at the high or low end if there are fewer cases in those ranges.

The key rule is that bins should not overlap. A person should fit into one and only one bin.

For continuous variables, the standard visual display is a histogram.

A histogram resembles a bar chart, but the bars touch. The touching bars indicate that the underlying variable is continuous. The boundaries between bins are artificial divisions imposed for display purposes.

For example, someone who is 39 years and 11 months old is very close to someone who is 40 years old. A histogram visually reflects that continuity by placing the bars next to one another.

The choice of bin width matters. If bins are too wide, we may hide important variation. If bins are too narrow, the graph may become noisy and difficult to interpret.

4. Describing Distributions

Once we have examined a distribution, we usually want to summarize it.

Descriptive statistics generally fall into two main categories:

  1. Measures of central tendency
  2. Measures of dispersion

A measure of central tendency describes what is typical, central, or common in a distribution.

The three most common measures of central tendency are:

  • mean
  • median
  • mode

A measure of dispersion describes how spread out the data are.

Common measures of dispersion include:

  • range
  • interquartile range
  • variance
  • standard deviation

Not every measure is appropriate for every variable. The measurement level of the variable determines what summaries make sense.

For example, the mean of income is meaningful. The mean of political party identification, if party is coded as Democrat = 1, Republican = 2, Independent = 3, is not meaningful. Statistical software may calculate it anyway, but the result does not make conceptual sense.

Software will often do what you ask, even when you should not have asked. The responsibility remains with the researcher.

Part I: Measures of Central Tendency

5. Central Tendency

A measure of central tendency describes what is typical in a distribution.

In everyday language, people often use the word “average” loosely. In statistics, we should be more precise. The word “average” can refer to different ideas, so it is better to specify whether we mean the mean, median, or mode.

These three measures capture different aspects of typicality:

  • The mean is the arithmetic center.
  • The median is the middle value in a rank ordering.
  • The mode is the most common value.

Sometimes these three measures are the same. Often, they are not.

6. The Mean

The mean is the arithmetic center of a distribution.

It is the value we get by adding up all observations and dividing by the number of observations.

For a population, the mean is represented by the Greek letter mu:

[ ]

For a sample, the mean is usually represented by placing a bar over the variable name:

[ {Y} ]

The mean is appropriate for interval- or ratio-level variables. It is not appropriate for nominal variables, and it is only sometimes used for ordinal variables when researchers are willing to treat the ordinal scale as approximately interval-level.

7. Summation Notation

To express the formula for the mean, we need summation notation.

Suppose we have a variable (Y), and each case has a value of that variable:

[ Y_1, Y_2, Y_3, , Y_N ]

The subscript identifies the case. (Y_1) is the value of (Y) for the first case, (Y_2) for the second case, and so on.

The Greek capital letter sigma,

So this expression:

[ _{i=1}^{N} Y_i ]

means:

[ Y_1 + Y_2 + Y_3 + + Y_N ]

In words: add up all the values of (Y) from the first case through the final case.

8. The Population Mean

  • (Y_i) is the value of (Y) for case (i)
  • (N) is the size of the population
  • (_Y) is the population mean of (Y)

The mean can also be understood as an expected value.

The expected value of (Y), written (E(Y)), is the value we would expect on average if we randomly selected a case from the population.

For a finite population where each case is equally likely to be selected:

[ E(Y) = _{i=1}^{N} Y_i ]

Because each case has probability (1/N), this simplifies to the familiar formula for the mean:

[ E(Y) = _{i=1}^{N} Y_i ]

Thus:

[ E(Y) = _Y ]

The mean is the probability-weighted center of the distribution.

This idea will become important later. For example, if we roll a fair six-sided die, the possible values are 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, and 6. Each has probability (1/6). The expected value is:

[ E(Y) = 1() + 2() + 3() + 4() + 5() + 6() ]

[ E(Y) = 3.5 ]

Notice that 3.5 is not a possible die roll. We cannot roll a 3.5. But 3.5 is still the expected value, because it is the mathematical center of the probability distribution.

9. The Sample Mean

Usually, we do not observe the full population. Instead, we have a sample.

For a sample of size (n), the sample mean is:

Equivalently:

This distinction is important:

  • (_Y) is the true mean in the population.
  • ({Y}) is the mean in the sample.
  • We usually use ({Y}) to estimate (_Y).

10. The Median

The median is the middle value in an ordered distribution.

To find the median, we rank the data from lowest to highest and identify the value that divides the data in half. Fifty percent of the observations lie at or below the median, and fifty percent lie at or above it.

The median is the 50th percentile.

A percentile is a position in a distribution below which a certain percentage of observations fall. For example:

  • The 25th percentile is the point below which 25 percent of the data fall.
  • The 50th percentile is the point below which 50 percent of the data fall.
  • The 75th percentile is the point below which 75 percent of the data fall.

The median can be used for ordinal, interval, and ratio variables. It cannot be used for nominal variables because nominal categories have no inherent order.

  3. Find the value associated with that position.

If the sample size is even, the median lies halfway between the two middle observations.

12. The Mode

The mode is the most common value in a distribution.

For example, if the most common response to a survey question is “Never,” then “Never” is the mode.

The mode is the only measure of central tendency that can be used with nominal variables. It can also be used with ordinal and interval-level variables.

The mode captures a different idea of typicality than the mean or median. It tells us what value appears most often, not where the mathematical center or rank-order middle lies.

A distribution can have:

  • one mode
  • more than one mode
  • no meaningful mode

For continuous variables, the mode can be less useful. If values are measured very precisely, many observations may have unique values. In that case, the “most common” value may be arbitrary or nonexistent.

13. Which Measures of Central Tendency Are Available?

The measurement level of the variable determines which measures of central tendency make sense.

Measurement Level Mean Median Mode
Nominal No No Yes
Ordinal Usually no Yes Yes
Interval/Ratio Yes Yes Yes

For nominal variables, only the mode is meaningful.

For ordinal variables, the median and mode are meaningful. The mean is usually not appropriate unless we are willing to treat the ordinal scale as approximately interval-level.

For interval and ratio variables, all three are available, though the mode may be less informative for continuous variables.

14. Comparing the Mean, Median, and Mode

The mean, median, and mode often tell different stories about the same distribution.

The mean is sensitive to extreme values. If a distribution contains very large or very small outliers, the mean can be pulled toward those outliers.

For example, imagine calculating the average wealth of people in a classroom. If everyone in the room has modest wealth, the mean may give a reasonable sense of typical wealth. But if Elon Musk walks into the room, the mean wealth skyrockets. The mean would no longer describe the typical person very well.

The median is less sensitive to extreme values. Adding one extremely wealthy person does not dramatically change the middle position in the rank ordering.

This is why income is often described using the median rather than the mean. Income distributions are usually positively skewed, with a small number of very high-income households pulling the mean upward. Median household income often gives a better sense of the typical household.

The mode identifies the most common value. For categorical variables, this may be the most useful summary. For example, if the most common religious service attendance category is “Never,” that tells us something important about the sample, even though it is not a mathematical center.

The best measure depends on the variable and the purpose of the description.

Part II: Measures of Dispersion

15. Dispersion

Measures of central tendency tell us what is typical. Measures of dispersion tell us how spread out the data are.

Two distributions can have the same mean but very different levels of dispersion.

For example, consider two classes with the same average exam score of 80.

In Class A, most students scored between 78 and 82.
In Class B, some students scored near 50 and others near 100.

Both classes have the same mean, but Class B has much more dispersion.

Common measures of dispersion include:

  • range
  • interquartile range
  • variance
  • standard deviation

As with central tendency, the appropriate measure depends on the measurement level of the variable.

16. Dispersion for Nominal Variables

For nominal variables, most formal measures of dispersion do not apply.

Nominal categories have no inherent order and no meaningful distance between categories. Therefore, we cannot calculate a range, interquartile range, variance, or standard deviation.

Instead, we describe the distribution qualitatively.

For example, we might say:

  • The data are concentrated in one category.
  • The data are evenly distributed across categories.
  • Two categories dominate the distribution.
  • Several categories are rare.

Suppose a religious affiliation variable has 70 percent of respondents in one category and very few in the others. We would say the distribution is highly concentrated. If each category has roughly the same percentage, we would say the distribution is more evenly dispersed.

This is still a description of dispersion, but it is not based on mathematical distance.

17. The Range

The range is the distance between the highest and lowest observed values in the data.

For interval or ratio variables:

[ = - ]

For example, if the youngest person in a sample is 18 and the oldest is 74, the range is:

[ 74 - 18 = 56 ]

The range is simple and easy to understand, but it is crude. It depends entirely on the two most extreme values.

If we add one very old person to an age distribution, the range may increase dramatically. If we remove one extreme observation, the range may shrink dramatically. Because of this, the range can be highly sensitive to outliers.

For ordinal variables, the range can be described qualitatively rather than numerically. For example, responses might range from “Strongly disagree” to “Strongly agree.” We generally should not treat that as a numeric distance unless the ordinal scale is being treated as approximately interval-level.

Also note that the range refers to the observed data, not the full possible range of the variable. Human age can theoretically extend beyond 100, but if the oldest person in our sample is 83, then 83 is the maximum observed value.

18. Percentiles and Quantiles

To understand the interquartile range, we need to return to percentiles.

A percentile identifies a position in a distribution. The 30th percentile is the value below which 30 percent of the data fall. The 80th percentile is the value below which 80 percent of the data fall.

Some percentiles are especially important.

The 25th percentile is called the first quartile, or (Q_1).

The 50th percentile is the median, or (Q_2).

The 75th percentile is called the third quartile, or (Q_3).

A quartile is a position, not a range. (Q_1) is the point that separates the lowest 25 percent of the data from the upper 75 percent. (Q_3) is the point that separates the lowest 75 percent from the upper 25 percent.

More generally, when we divide data into equal-sized parts, we are creating quantiles.

Examples include:

  • quartiles: four equal parts
  • quintiles: five equal parts
  • deciles: ten equal parts
  • percentiles: one hundred equal parts

Quantiles help us describe where observations fall in a distribution.

19. The Interquartile Range

The interquartile range, or IQR, measures the spread of the middle 50 percent of the data.

It is the distance between the 75th percentile and the 25th percentile:

[ IQR = Q_3 - Q_1 ]

The IQR tells us how far we must go on the variable’s scale to cover the middle half of the distribution.

If the IQR is small, the middle 50 percent of the data are tightly clustered. If the IQR is large, the middle 50 percent are more spread out.

The IQR is less sensitive to extreme values than the range. Adding a very large or very small outlier may greatly affect the range, but it usually has little effect on the IQR.

This makes the IQR especially useful for skewed distributions or distributions with outliers.

20. Finding the Interquartile Range

To find the IQR:

  1. Sort the data from lowest to highest.
  2. Find the median.
  3. Split the data into a lower half and an upper half.
  4. Find the median of the lower half. This is (Q_1).
  5. Find the median of the upper half. This is (Q_3).
  6. Subtract:

[ IQR = Q_3 - Q_1 ]

There are slightly different conventions for how to handle the middle observation when the sample size is odd. Some methods include the median in both halves; others exclude it from both halves. With small samples, this can produce slightly different answers. With large samples, the difference is usually negligible.

Example: IQR for Age

Suppose we have the ages of 13 graduates:

[ 24, 24, 25, 25, 26, 26, 27, 28, 28, 29, 31, 34, 37 ]

First, find the median.

There are 13 observations, so the median position is:

[ = 7 ]

The seventh observation is 27, so the median is 27.

Now split the data into lower and upper halves, including the median in both halves:

Lower half:

[ 24, 24, 25, 25, 26, 26, 27 ]

Upper half:

[ 27, 28, 28, 29, 31, 34, 37 ]

Now find the median of each half.

The median of the lower half is 25, so:

[ Q_1 = 25 ]

The median of the upper half is 29, so:

[ Q_3 = 29 ]

Therefore:

[ IQR = 29 - 25 = 4 ]

The middle 50 percent of the age distribution spans four years.

For ordinal variables, the IQR can still be useful, but we should interpret it qualitatively rather than as a precise numeric distance.

22. Box-and-Whisker Plots

The interquartile range often appears in a graph called a box-and-whisker plot, or simply a box plot.

A box plot usually displays:

  • the median
  • (Q_1)
  • (Q_3)
  • the interquartile range
  • whiskers showing additional spread
  • sometimes individual outliers

The box itself represents the middle 50 percent of the data. The line inside the box marks the median.

The whiskers may be defined in different ways. In some graphs, they extend to the minimum and maximum values. In others, they extend to values within 1.5 times the IQR, with points beyond that shown as outliers.

Box plots are useful for comparing distributions across groups.

For example, suppose we compare the heights of soccer players by position. Goalkeepers may have a higher median height and a smaller IQR, meaning they are generally tall and relatively clustered in height. Defenders may show a wider IQR, indicating more variation. A box plot allows us to see these differences quickly.

Part III: Variance and Standard Deviation

23. Variability Around the Mean

The range and IQR describe spread using positions in the distribution. Variance and standard deviation describe spread around the mean.

These measures apply to interval- and ratio-level variables. They are not appropriate for nominal variables. They are also generally not appropriate for ordinal variables unless the researcher is treating the ordinal scale as approximately interval-level.

The central question is:

How far are observations, typically, from the mean?

If most observations are close to the mean, dispersion is low. If many observations are far from the mean, dispersion is high.

For each observation, we can calculate its deviation from the mean:

If the deviation is positive, the observation is above the mean.
If the deviation is negative, the observation is below the mean.

[ 5, 12, 6, 9, 3 ]

[ -2 + 5 - 1 + 2 - 4 = 0 ]

That creates a problem. If we try to calculate the average deviation from the mean, the positive and negative deviations cancel out.

So we need a way to make deviations positive before averaging them.

One option would be to take absolute values. That produces a statistic called the mean absolute deviation. But for reasons that become important in more advanced statistics, squared deviations have better mathematical properties.

So instead of taking absolute values, we square the deviations.

29. Why Do We Divide by (n - 1)?

When calculating the population variance, we divide by (N). When calculating the sample variance, we divide by (n - 1).

Why?

Because the sample variance is used to estimate the population variance. We do not know the population mean (), so we use the sample mean ({Y}) instead.

But ({Y}) is itself estimated from the same sample data. Once we use the data to estimate the mean, we lose one independent piece of information for estimating variability around that mean.

This is the idea of degrees of freedom.

The sample has (n) observations, but after estimating the sample mean, only (n - 1) observations are free to vary independently around that mean.

30. Degrees of Freedom

Degrees of freedom can be difficult at first, so consider a simple example.

Suppose we have two observations:

[ 2, 4 ]

The mean is:

[ {Y} = 3 ]

Now suppose you know the mean is 3 and I tell you that one observation is 2. What must the other observation be?

It must be 4.

It is not free to be anything else, because the two values must average to 3.

With two observations and a known sample mean, only one observation is free to vary. The other is determined.

That is why, when estimating variance around a sample mean, we have:

[ n - 1 ]

degrees of freedom.

The same logic applies to larger samples. If we have 100 observations and we know the sample mean, then once 99 values are known, the final value is fixed. It must be whatever value makes the mean come out correctly.

So when calculating sample variance, we divide by (n - 1) to account for the fact that we already used the data to estimate the mean.

31. The Correction Matters Most in Small Samples

Dividing by (n - 1) rather than (n) makes the estimate of variance slightly larger.

This corrects a tendency for samples to underestimate population variability. A sample is unlikely to capture the full spread of the population, especially when the sample is small. It may miss extreme values in the tails of the distribution.

Using (n - 1) inflates the estimate slightly, making it a better estimator of the population variance.

The correction matters more when the sample is small.

For example, dividing by 6 instead of 7 makes a noticeable difference. But dividing by 1,499 instead of 1,500 makes almost no difference.

As sample size increases, the distinction between (n) and (n - 1) becomes less important.

Part IV: Skewness

33. Skewness

A distribution is skewed if it is asymmetric.

A symmetric distribution has the same general shape on both sides. A classic example is the normal distribution, or bell curve. In a perfectly symmetric normal distribution, the mean, median, and mode all line up.

A skewed distribution has a longer tail on one side than the other.

There are two main types of skewness:

  1. Positive skew, also called right skew
  2. Negative skew, also called left skew

34. Positive Skew

A distribution has positive skew if it has a long tail extending toward higher values.

In a positively skewed distribution, most observations are concentrated toward the lower end, while a smaller number of observations stretch far to the right.

Income is a common example. Most households earn relatively moderate incomes, while a smaller number earn extremely high incomes. Those high-income observations create a long right tail.

In positively skewed distributions, the mean is often greater than the median because the high values pull the mean upward.

A distribution has negative skew if it has a long tail extending toward lower values.

In a negatively skewed distribution, most observations are concentrated toward the higher end, while a smaller number stretch far to the left.

36. Skewness and Choice of Summary Statistic

Skewness matters because it affects how we describe what is typical.

When a distribution is symmetric, the mean and median may be similar. In that case, either may provide a reasonable summary of central tendency.

When a distribution is skewed, the mean and median can differ substantially.

Consider household income. Suppose the median household income is:

while the mean household income is:

[ $79,263 ]

These numbers tell different stories.

The mean is higher because high-income households pull it upward. The median tells us the income of the household in the middle of the distribution. Half of households earn less than the median, and half earn more.

If we want to describe the typical household, the median is usually more informative. If we want to calculate total income divided equally across households, the mean may be appropriate.

Neither statistic is inherently wrong. They answer different questions.

37. Reading a Skewed Distribution

When examining a distribution, ask:

  1. Is the distribution symmetric?
  2. Is there a long tail?
  3. If so, does the tail extend toward higher or lower values?
  4. Are there outliers?
  5. How different are the mean and median?
  6. Which measure of central tendency best communicates what is typical?

A positively skewed distribution has a long right tail.
A negatively skewed distribution has a long left tail.

The name of the skew refers to the direction of the tail.

This is easy to mix up. Remember: the skew points toward the tail, not toward where most of the data are concentrated.

Part V: Choosing Appropriate Descriptive Statistics

38. Matching Statistics to Measurement Level

The most important practical rule is that descriptive statistics must match the measurement level of the variable.

Variable Type Useful Displays Central Tendency Dispersion
Nominal Frequency table, bar chart Mode Qualitative description
Ordinal Frequency table, bar chart Median, mode Range, IQR by category
Interval/Ratio, discrete Frequency table, bar chart, histogram if many values Mean, median, mode Range, IQR, variance, standard deviation
Interval/Ratio, continuous Histogram, box plot Mean, median, sometimes mode Range, IQR, variance, standard deviation

Software may compute statistics that are not meaningful. A researcher must know when a statistic is appropriate.

For example:

  • A mean of income is meaningful.
  • A mean of religious affiliation is not meaningful.
  • A median of ideology may be meaningful if ideology is ordered.
  • A standard deviation of party identification is not meaningful if party categories are just labels.
  • A mode can be used for almost any variable, but may be unhelpful for continuous data.

39. Descriptive Statistics as Interpretation

Descriptive statistics are not just mechanical calculations. They are choices about how to represent data.

Suppose we describe income using the mean. We emphasize total income divided across households. Suppose we describe it using the median. We emphasize the household in the middle. Suppose we describe it using a histogram. We show the entire shape of the distribution, including skewness and outliers.

Each description is true in a narrow sense, but each highlights different features.

Good descriptive analysis often uses multiple tools:

  • a graph to show the shape of the distribution
  • a measure of central tendency to identify what is typical
  • a measure of dispersion to show spread
  • attention to skewness and outliers
  • careful interpretation based on the measurement level of the variable

Descriptive statistics are the foundation for later statistical analysis. If we do not understand the basic structure of the data, we are not ready to model relationships or test hypotheses.

Chapter Summary

Descriptive statistics allow us to summarize and understand data before moving to more advanced analysis. The first step is to examine a variable’s distribution: the pattern of values in the sample or population.

Categorical and discrete variables are often summarized using frequency tables and bar charts. Continuous variables are often summarized using histograms, where values are grouped into bins.

Measures of central tendency describe what is typical. The mean is the arithmetic center, the median is the middle value in a rank ordering, and the mode is the most common value. Which measure is appropriate depends on the measurement level of the variable and the shape of the distribution.

Measures of dispersion describe how spread out the data are. The range captures the distance between the minimum and maximum observed values. The interquartile range captures the spread of the middle 50 percent. Variance and standard deviation measure spread around the mean.

The standard deviation is especially important because it appears throughout statistical analysis. It is based on squared deviations from the mean and, for sample data, is calculated using (n - 1) degrees of freedom.

Finally, skewness describes asymmetry. Positively skewed distributions have long right tails; negatively skewed distributions have long left tails. Skewness affects how we interpret the mean, median, and mode. In skewed distributions, the median often gives a better sense of what is typical than the mean.

The central lesson is that descriptive statistics are not merely calculations. They are tools for representing data accurately. Good analysis begins by choosing the right summaries for the right variables.
