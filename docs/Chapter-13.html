Chapter 13: Correlation

Correlation analysis is our first major tool for studying relationships between variables when all variables in the analysis are measured at the interval or ratio level.

Until now, most of our analyses have involved at least one categorical variable. We compared means across categories, compared proportions across groups, used chi-square tests for categorical association, and used ANOVA to compare multiple group means. Correlation analysis is different. It is used when both variables are quantitative.

For example, we might ask:

  • Are wealthier countries more democratic?
  • Do countries that spend more on education have higher life expectancy?
  • Is unionization related to economic inequality?
  • Do students who study more hours earn higher exam scores?
  • Is unemployment related to crime?
  • Are years of education related to income?

Correlation analysis measures the strength and direction of the relationship between two interval-level variables.

The main statistic we use is Pearson’s correlation coefficient, usually written as (r). Pearson’s (r) measures the degree to which two variables have a linear relationship.

The central idea is simple:

Correlation tells us whether two quantitative variables tend to move together, move in opposite directions, or show little linear relationship.

Part I: Preliminaries

1. When Do We Use Correlation?

Correlation is used when the variables in the analysis are interval- or ratio-level variables.

Examples include:

  • income
  • age
  • life expectancy
  • unemployment rate
  • education spending
  • GDP per capita
  • test scores
  • temperature
  • unionization rate
  • inequality measures
  • democracy scores, if treated as interval-level

When variables are categorical, we use other tools, such as:

  • difference-of-means tests
  • difference-of-proportions tests
  • chi-square tests
  • ANOVA

But when both variables are quantitative, correlation is often a useful first step.

Because interval-level variables can take many values, we usually do not create joint frequency tables. There would be too many possible combinations. Instead, we use scatterplots and correlation coefficients.

2. Scatterplots

A scatterplot displays the relationship between two quantitative variables.

One variable is placed on the horizontal axis, usually called (X). The other is placed on the vertical axis, usually called (Y).

Each point represents one case.

For example, if we are studying countries:

  • (X): democracy score
  • (Y): life expectancy

Each dot on the scatterplot is a country.

Scatterplots help us see whether the relationship is:

  • positive
  • negative
  • weak
  • strong
  • linear
  • nonlinear
  • influenced by outliers

Correlation analysis gives us a numerical summary of what we see visually in a scatterplot.

3. Strength and Direction

Correlation describes two features of a relationship:

  1. Direction
  2. Strength

Direction

A relationship is positive if higher values of one variable tend to be associated with higher values of the other variable.

For example, if countries with higher education spending tend to have higher life expectancy, the relationship is positive.

A relationship is negative if higher values of one variable tend to be associated with lower values of the other variable.

For example, if countries with higher unionization tend to have lower economic inequality, the relationship is negative.

Important point:

If both variables decrease together, the relationship is positive, not negative.

Positive means the variables move in the same direction. Negative means they move in opposite directions.

Strength

Strength refers to how closely the data follow a linear pattern.

If the points in a scatterplot fall almost exactly along a straight line, the relationship is strong.

If the points are widely scattered, the relationship is weak.

Part II: Pearson’s Correlation Coefficient

4. Pearson’s (r)

The most common measure of correlation is Pearson’s correlation coefficient, written:

[ r ]

Pearson’s (r) measures the strength and direction of a linear relationship between two variables.

It ranges from:

[ -1 r ]

The interpretation is:

Value of (r) Interpretation
(r = 1) Perfect positive linear relationship
(r = 0) No linear relationship
(r = -1) Perfect negative linear relationship

Values between these extremes indicate weaker or stronger relationships.

For example:

  • (r = 0.80): strong positive linear relationship
  • (r = 0.30): weak-to-moderate positive linear relationship
  • (r = -0.60): moderately strong negative linear relationship
  • (r = -0.05): very weak negative linear relationship
  • (r = 0.00): no linear relationship

The closer (r) is to 1 or -1, the stronger the linear relationship.

The closer (r) is to 0, the weaker the linear relationship.

5. Positive Correlation

A positive correlation means that higher values of (X) tend to be associated with higher values of (Y).

In a scatterplot, the points tend to slope upward from left to right.

For example, suppose countries with higher democracy scores tend to have higher life expectancy. Then the correlation between democracy and life expectancy would be positive.

A perfect positive correlation would mean all points fall exactly on an upward-sloping line:

[ r = 1 ]

In real data, perfect correlation is rare. Social data are wonderfully messy, because humans insist on being complicated.

6. Negative Correlation

A negative correlation means that higher values of (X) tend to be associated with lower values of (Y).

In a scatterplot, the points tend to slope downward from left to right.

For example, suppose countries with higher unionization rates tend to have lower economic inequality. Then the correlation between unionization and inequality would be negative.

A perfect negative correlation would mean all points fall exactly on a downward-sloping line:

[ r = -1 ]

Again, perfect negative correlation is rare in social science data.

7. No Linear Correlation

If (r) is close to 0, the variables have little or no linear relationship.

A scatterplot with no linear correlation may look like a random cloud of points.

However, this is an important caution:

A correlation of zero means there is no linear relationship. It does not necessarily mean there is no relationship at all.

Two variables can be related in a nonlinear way.

For example, suppose the relationship between age and happiness is U-shaped: happiness is higher among young adults, lower in middle age, and higher again later in life. Pearson’s (r) might be close to zero because the positive and negative parts of the relationship cancel out, even though a real nonlinear relationship exists.

Pearson’s (r) measures linearity. It does not detect every kind of relationship.

Part III: Interpreting Pearson’s (r)

8. Magnitude and Direction

Suppose we calculate:

[ r = 0.77 ]

This indicates a strong positive linear relationship.

Suppose instead:

[ r = -0.60 ]

This indicates a moderately strong negative linear relationship.

Suppose:

[ r = -0.01 ]

This indicates essentially no linear relationship.

The sign tells us direction.

The absolute value tells us strength.

So:

[ r = -0.80 ]

is stronger than:

[ r = 0.30 ]

even though one is negative and one is positive.

9. Correlation Does Not Measure Slope

Pearson’s (r) does not tell us how much (Y) changes when (X) changes.

It tells us how closely the data fit a straight line.

A correlation of 1 can occur for any positive slope, as long as the points fall perfectly on a line.

For example, both of these could have:

[ r = 1 ]

  • a line where (Y) increases by 1 for every 1-unit increase in (X)
  • a line where (Y) increases by 100 for every 1-unit increase in (X)

Correlation tells us linear strength and direction, not the magnitude of the change.

Regression will add that next piece by estimating a slope.

10. Correlation and Causation

Correlation does not establish causation.

If two variables are correlated, several possibilities remain:

  • (X) causes (Y)
  • (Y) causes (X)
  • a third variable causes both
  • the relationship is spurious
  • the relationship reflects selection effects
  • the relationship is driven by outliers
  • the relationship is coincidental

For example, if democracy and life expectancy are positively correlated, democracy may contribute to better health outcomes. But wealth, education, health systems, state capacity, or regional history may also matter.

Correlation is useful for describing relationships. It is not enough to prove causality.

Part IV: How Pearson’s (r) Works

11. The Logic of the Formula

The formula for Pearson’s (r) is:

[ r = { } ]

You do not need to memorize or calculate this formula by hand.

But understanding the logic helps.

The numerator measures covariation.

The denominator rescales that covariation by the total variation in (X) and (Y).

In rough terms:

[ r { X Y} ]

The result is a standardized measure bounded between -1 and 1.

12. Deviations from the Mean

The formula asks whether (X) and (Y) tend to be above or below their means at the same time.

For each case, we calculate:

[ X_i - {X} ]

and:

[ Y_i - {Y} ]

These are deviations from the mean.

If (X_i) is above its mean, then:

[ X_i-{X} > 0 ]

If (X_i) is below its mean, then:

[ X_i-{X} < 0 ]

The same logic applies to (Y).

The key is the product:

[ (X_i-{X})(Y_i-{Y}) ]

This product is positive when (X) and (Y) are on the same side of their means.

It is negative when they are on opposite sides.

13. Positive Contributions

A case contributes positive evidence for correlation when:

  1. (X) is above its mean and (Y) is above its mean.
  2. (X) is below its mean and (Y) is below its mean.

In the first case:

[ (+)(+) = + ]

In the second case:

[ (-)(-) = + ]

These cases suggest that (X) and (Y) move together.

14. Negative Contributions

A case contributes negative evidence for correlation when:

  1. (X) is above its mean and (Y) is below its mean.
  2. (X) is below its mean and (Y) is above its mean.

In the first case:

[ (+)(-) = - ]

In the second case:

[ (-)(+) = - ]

These cases suggest that (X) and (Y) move in opposite directions.

15. A Small Numerical Example

Suppose we have four cases:

Case (X) (Y)
1 6 8
2 3 7
3 4 4
4 5 7

Suppose:

[ {X} = 4 ]

and:

[ {Y} = 6 ]

Now calculate deviations:

Case (X-{X}) (Y-{Y}) Product
1 2 2 4
2 -1 1 -1
3 0 -2 0
4 1 1 1

The products sum to:

[ 4 + (-1) + 0 + 1 = 4 ]

The sum is positive, meaning the overall covariation is positive.

This suggests a positive correlation between (X) and (Y).

16. Quadrants in a Scatterplot

A scatterplot can help us visualize the same logic.

Draw a vertical line at ({X}) and a horizontal line at ({Y}). This divides the scatterplot into four quadrants.

Upper-right quadrant

(X) is above its mean.
(Y) is above its mean.
This contributes positive correlation.

Lower-left quadrant

(X) is below its mean.
(Y) is below its mean.
This also contributes positive correlation.

Upper-left quadrant

(X) is below its mean.
(Y) is above its mean.
This contributes negative correlation.

Lower-right quadrant

(X) is above its mean.
(Y) is below its mean.
This contributes negative correlation.

If most points fall in the upper-right and lower-left quadrants, the correlation is positive.

If most points fall in the upper-left and lower-right quadrants, the correlation is negative.

If points are scattered evenly across all quadrants, the correlation is near zero.

20. Example: Democracy and Life Expectancy

Suppose we calculate the correlation between democracy and female life expectancy across countries.

The result is:

[ r = 0.3751 ]

This indicates a positive relationship: more democratic countries tend to have higher female life expectancy.

The relationship is not extremely strong, but it is clearly positive.

If the p-value is very small, we reject:

[ H_0: = 0 ]

and conclude that the correlation is statistically significant.

A careful interpretation is:

Democracy and female life expectancy are positively correlated. The relationship is statistically significant, though the magnitude of the correlation is moderate.

Again, this does not by itself prove that democracy causes higher life expectancy.

Part VI: Using Software

21. Correlation Commands

Correlation is usually calculated with software.

In Stata

A common command is:

pwcorr var1 var2, sig

The option:

sig

requests p-values.

If you want sample sizes as well, you can use:

pwcorr var1 var2, sig obs

In R

The base R command is:

cor.test(var1, var2)

For multiple variables, you can use:

cor(data[, c("var1", "var2", "var3", "var4")],
    use = "pairwise.complete.obs")

The option:

use = "pairwise.complete.obs"

tells R to use all available data for each pair of variables.

This matters when some variables have missing values. Without this option, R may drop any row with missing data on any variable in the correlation matrix, even if the pair currently being analyzed has no missing values.

For fuller output, including p-values and sample sizes, the corr.test function in the psych package is useful.

22. Correlation Matrices

When we calculate correlations among several variables, software reports a correlation matrix.

A correlation matrix has the same variables in the rows and columns.

For example:

Variable 1 Variable 2 Variable 3 Variable 4
Variable 1 1.00 (r_{12}) (r_{13}) (r_{14})
Variable 2 (r_{21}) 1.00 (r_{23}) (r_{24})
Variable 3 (r_{31}) (r_{32}) 1.00 (r_{34})
Variable 4 (r_{41}) (r_{42}) (r_{43}) 1.00

The diagonal values are always 1 because each variable is perfectly correlated with itself.

The matrix is symmetric:

[ r_{12} = r_{21} ]

[ r_{13} = r_{31} ]

and so on.

Some software reports the full matrix. Other software reports only one triangle of the matrix to avoid duplication.

23. Example: Cross-National Correlations

Suppose we analyze several country-level variables:

  • autocracy score
  • education spending as a percentage of GDP
  • unemployment rate
  • Gini coefficient, a measure of income inequality
  • unionization rate

A correlation matrix might show that the correlation between unionization and inequality is:

[ r = -0.43 ]

This is a moderate negative relationship.

Interpretation:

Countries with higher unionization rates tend to have lower income inequality.

This is a descriptive association. It does not prove that unionization causes lower inequality. Other factors may influence both.

Suppose the correlation between autocracy and education spending is:

[ r = -0.08 ]

This is a very weak negative relationship.

Interpretation:

More autocratic countries tend to spend slightly less on education, but the relationship is weak.

Always interpret both direction and magnitude.

Part VII: Cautions in Correlation Analysis

24. Correlation Measures Linear Association

Pearson’s (r) measures linear relationships.

If the true relationship is curved, U-shaped, threshold-based, or otherwise nonlinear, Pearson’s (r) may be misleading.

Always look at a scatterplot when possible.

A correlation coefficient is useful, but it should not be the only thing you inspect.

25. Outliers Can Matter

Outliers can strongly influence correlations.

A single extreme case may make a relationship appear stronger, weaker, or even reverse its direction.

For example, a very wealthy country with unusual political institutions may influence a cross-national correlation between democracy and income.

In later regression analysis, we will return to outliers more directly. For now, the basic advice is:

Do not rely on Pearson’s (r) alone. Look at the data.

26. Correlation Does Not Control for Other Variables

Correlation is bivariate. It examines the relationship between two variables at a time.

It does not control for other factors.

For example, suppose democracy and life expectancy are correlated. That relationship might change after controlling for GDP per capita.

Correlation cannot tell us whether the relationship remains after accounting for other variables.

Regression analysis allows us to include controls.

Correlation is a useful starting point, not the final word.

27. Statistical Significance Is Not Substantive Significance

With a large sample, even a weak correlation can be statistically significant.

For example:

[ r = 0.05 ]

may be statistically significant if (n) is very large.

But it may not be substantively meaningful.

Always ask:

  • Is the relationship statistically significant?
  • How strong is the relationship?
  • Is the relationship meaningful in context?
  • Does the relationship make theoretical sense?

A small p-value does not make a weak relationship important.

Key Terms

Correlation
A measure of association between two variables.

Pearson’s (r)
The most common correlation coefficient, measuring the strength and direction of a linear relationship between two interval-level variables.

Positive correlation
A relationship in which higher values of one variable tend to be associated with higher values of another.

Negative correlation
A relationship in which higher values of one variable tend to be associated with lower values of another.

Linear relationship
A relationship that can be summarized by a straight line.

Scatterplot
A graph showing the relationship between two quantitative variables.

Covariation
The extent to which two variables vary together.

Correlation matrix
A table showing correlations among multiple variables.

Population correlation ()
The true correlation between two variables in the population.

Sample correlation (r)
The correlation between two variables calculated from sample data.

Standard error of (r)
The estimated sampling variability of Pearson’s (r).

Bivariate analysis
Analysis involving two variables.

Outlier
An observation with an unusually high or low value that may influence the analysis.

Chapter Summary

Correlation analysis measures the relationship between two interval-level variables.

The most common measure is Pearson’s correlation coefficient, (r). It ranges from -1 to 1. Positive values indicate that the variables tend to move together. Negative values indicate that they tend to move in opposite directions. Values near zero indicate little or no linear relationship.

Pearson’s (r) measures linear association. It does not detect all possible relationships, especially nonlinear ones. A correlation near zero does not necessarily mean two variables are unrelated; it means they are not linearly related.

The formula for Pearson’s (r) is based on covariation. It asks whether (X) and (Y) tend to be above or below their means at the same time. If they move together, the correlation is positive. If they move oppositely, it is negative.

We can test whether a sample correlation differs from zero using a t-test with (n-2) degrees of freedom. The standard error of (r) is:

[ SE_r = ]

Correlation matrices allow us to examine many pairwise relationships at once.

The central caution is that correlation is descriptive. It does not establish causality, does not control for other variables, and may miss nonlinear patterns. It is an important first step in analyzing relationships among quantitative variables, and it prepares us for the next major topic: linear regression.
