Chapter 12: ANOVA

We now turn to a method called ANOVA, which stands for analysis of variance.

ANOVA is used when we want to compare more than two means at the same time. If we have only two means, we can use a difference-of-means t-test. But if we have three, four, five, or more group means, running many separate t-tests becomes cumbersome and can create problems with repeated testing.

ANOVA gives us a way to ask one broader question:

Are the means across these groups all the same, or does at least one group differ?

ANOVA is not as widely used in some areas of modern applied statistics as regression, because regression can accomplish many of the same goals more flexibly. Still, ANOVA remains useful conceptually and practically. It helps us understand how variation in an outcome can be partitioned into variation explained by group differences and variation left unexplained within groups.

The central idea is this:

ANOVA compares variation between groups to variation within groups.

If between-group variation is large relative to within-group variation, then group membership helps explain the outcome. If between-group variation is small relative to within-group variation, then the group categories do not explain much.

1. Comparing Several Means

Suppose we have an interval-level dependent variable (Y), such as:

  • test score
  • income
  • life expectancy
  • thermometer rating
  • infant mortality rate
  • air quality index

And suppose we have a categorical independent variable (X), such as:

  • political party
  • school
  • classroom
  • region
  • regime type
  • treatment category

If (X) has only two categories, we can compare the two means using a t-test.

But if (X) has more than two categories, we need another approach.

For example, suppose we want to compare student test scores across five classrooms. The null hypothesis is that all five classrooms have the same mean test score. The alternative is that at least one classroom has a different mean.

This is precisely the kind of question ANOVA is designed to answer.

2. What Does ANOVA Ask?

ANOVA asks whether the means of an interval-level variable differ across categories of one or more categorical variables.

For a one-way ANOVA, we have:

  • one interval-level dependent variable, (Y)

  • 100 means very warm feelings.

The question is not whether the sample means differ. They plainly do. The question is whether those differences are large enough, relative to the variation within groups, to reject the null hypothesis that the population means are all the same.

Part I: The Logic of ANOVA

4. Analysis of Variance

ANOVA stands for analysis of variance.

That name may seem surprising because we are comparing means. But the method works by analyzing variance.

Specifically, ANOVA partitions the total variation in (Y) into two parts:

  1. Between-group variation
  2. Within-group variation

The key question is:

Is variation between group means large relative to variation within groups?

If the group means are far apart and individuals within each group are clustered close to their group mean, then group membership explains a lot of variation in (Y).

If the group means are close together or the groups overlap heavily, then group membership does not explain much variation in (Y).

5. Between-Group Variation

Between-group variation refers to variation in the group means.

In the Obama thermometer example, the group means are:

  • Democrats: 85.31
  • Independents: 55.70
  • Republicans: 26.62

These means are far apart.

That suggests party identification explains a substantial amount of variation in Obama thermometer scores.

Between-group variation asks:

How far are the group means from the overall mean?

The overall mean is:

[ {Y} = 60.72 ]

So we examine how far each group mean is from 60.72, weighting by group size.

6. Within-Group Variation

Within-group variation refers to variation among individuals inside the same group.

Not all Democrats rate Obama exactly the same way. Not all Republicans do either. There is variation within each party category.

For Democrats, the standard deviation is 19.10.
For Independents, it is 27.10.
For Republicans, it is 25.40.

Within-group variation asks:

How much do individuals vary around their own group means?

If within-group variation is large, then group membership may not explain much. If within-group variation is small, and group means are far apart, then group membership explains more.

7. The F Statistic

ANOVA uses an F statistic.

The F statistic is a ratio:

[ F = {} ]

If between-group variance is large relative to within-group variance, (F) will be large.

If between-group variance is small relative to within-group variance, (F) will be small.

The smallest possible F statistic is 0. That would occur when all group means are identical.

Because variances are always nonnegative, F statistics cannot be negative.

Like chi-square tests, ANOVA uses a one-sided test. We reject the null hypothesis only for large values of (F).

8. Visual Intuition

Imagine three groups.

In one scenario, each group is tightly clustered around a different mean. The group distributions barely overlap. Between-group variation is large, and within-group variation is small. ANOVA will produce a large F statistic.

In another scenario, the three group means differ slightly, but the observations within each group are widely spread out. The distributions overlap heavily. Between-group variation is small relative to within-group variation. ANOVA will produce a smaller F statistic.

Part II: Mathematical Details

9. Notation

Suppose we have:

  • total sample size (N)
  • number of groups (G)
  • sample size in group (g): (n_g)
  • outcome for person (i) in group (g): (Y_{ig})
  • mean of group (g): ({Y}_g)
  • overall mean: ({Y})

ANOVA compares the group means ({Y}_g) to the overall mean ({Y}), while also accounting for variation among individuals within each group.

10. Between Sum of Squares

The between sum of squares measures how far group means are from the overall mean, weighted by group size.

[ SS_{between} = _{g=1}^{G} n_g({Y}_g - {Y})^2 ]

For each group:

  1. Take the group mean.
  2. Subtract the overall mean.
  3. Square the difference.
  4. Multiply by the group size.
  5. Add across groups.

Groups with more cases contribute more to the between-group sum of squares.

The degrees of freedom for the between-group component are:

[ df_{between} = G - 1 ]

[ SS_{within} = {g=1}^{G} {i=1}^{n_g} (Y_{ig} - {Y}_g)^2 ]

[ df_{within} = N - G ]

Part IV: Another One-Way ANOVA Example

20. Test Scores Across Schools

Suppose we have math test scores for students in an urban school district.

There are:

  • 8,064 students
  • 25 schools

The question is:

Do mean math test scores differ across schools?

The null hypothesis is:

[ H_0: _1 = 2 = = {25} ]

The alternative is:

[ H_A: ]

A one-way ANOVA can test whether school membership explains statistically significant variation in test scores.

If the F statistic is large and the p-value is small, we reject the null hypothesis and conclude that mean test scores differ across schools.

This does not tell us why schools differ. Differences may reflect school quality, neighborhood inequality, student composition, resources, peer effects, or other factors. ANOVA tells us whether there are differences, not what caused them.

Part V: Two-Way ANOVA

21. Adding a Second Categorical Variable

A two-way ANOVA uses two categorical independent variables to explain variation in an interval-level dependent variable.

For example, suppose we want to explain Obama thermometer scores using:

  • party identification
  • region

Party ID has three categories:

  • Democrat
  • Independent
  • Republican

Region has two categories:

  • South
  • not South

Together, they create six combinations:

  1. Democrat, not South
  2. Democrat, South
  3. Independent, not South
  4. Independent, South
  5. Republican, not South
  6. Republican, South

Two-way ANOVA allows us to ask whether party matters after controlling for region, and whether region matters after controlling for party.

22. Example: Party ID, Region, and Obama Thermometer Scores

Suppose the table looks roughly like this:

Party ID Not South Mean South Mean Overall Mean
Democrat 82.0 86.2 85.3
Independent 54.2 51.5 55.7
Republican 27.1 24.7 26.6

We can ask several questions.

First:

Do all six means differ from one another?

That is the model-level question.

Second:

After controlling for region, does party ID still explain differences in Obama thermometer scores?

Third:

After controlling for party ID, does region still explain differences in Obama thermometer scores?

ANOVA can provide F tests for each question.

23. Interpreting Two-Way ANOVA Output

A two-way ANOVA output may include rows such as:

Source F p-value
Model large <0.001
Party ID very large <0.001
Region smaller 0.04
Residual

The model row tests whether the full set of categories, taken together, explains variation in (Y).

The Party ID row tests whether party ID explains variation in (Y), after controlling for region.

The Region row tests whether region explains variation in (Y), after controlling for party ID.

If party ID has a very small p-value, then party ID matters even after controlling for region.

If region has a p-value of 0.04, then region is statistically significant at the 0.05 level, but we still need to ask whether the effect is substantively meaningful.

In the example, regional differences are small compared to party differences. With a large sample, even small differences may become statistically significant.

Statistical significance and substantive importance are not the same thing.

24. Controlling for Variables in ANOVA

Two-way ANOVA introduces the idea of statistical control.

When we say:

Party ID matters after controlling for region,

we mean that differences across party categories remain even when we compare people within the same region category.

Likewise:

Region matters after controlling for party ID

means that regional differences remain even when we compare people within the same party category.

This is conceptually similar to controlled mean comparison tables. ANOVA gives us a formal significance test for those comparisons.

Part VI: Democracy, Wealth, and Life Expectancy

25. The Research Question

Suppose we are interested in whether democracies have higher life expectancy than non-democracies.

The dependent variable is:

  • life expectancy

The categorical independent variables are:

  • regime type: democracy or non-democracy
  • GDP per capita category: low, middle, high

We know that democracies tend to be wealthier than non-democracies. Wealthier countries tend to have better health systems, infrastructure, nutrition, education, and public services.

So GDP per capita may confound the relationship between democracy and life expectancy.

A two-way ANOVA can help us ask:

  1. Does regime type explain life expectancy after controlling for GDP category?
  2. Does GDP category explain life expectancy after controlling for regime type?

26. Mean Comparison Table

Suppose the table is:

Regime Type Low GDP Middle GDP High GDP Overall
Non-democracy 57.8 68.7 73.5 64.5
Democracy 63.3 74.1 79.2 72.5

The table suggests that democracies have higher life expectancy within each income category.

It also suggests that higher-income countries have higher life expectancy within both regime categories.

A two-way ANOVA allows us to attach statistical significance to these patterns.

27. Two-Way ANOVA Interpretation

Suppose ANOVA output shows:

Source F p-value
Model 62.32 <0.001
Democracy 21.81 <0.001
GDP category 65.33 <0.001

The model as a whole is statistically significant.

Democracy remains statistically significant after controlling for GDP per capita category.

GDP per capita category remains statistically significant after controlling for democracy.

A careful interpretation is:

We reject the null hypothesis that mean life expectancy is the same in democracies and non-democracies after controlling for GDP per capita category. We also reject the null hypothesis that mean life expectancy is the same across GDP categories after controlling for regime type.

This does not automatically prove that democracy causes higher life expectancy. It shows that the association remains after controlling for a coarse measure of country wealth.

Causality would require stronger design or additional controls.

Part VII: ANOVA and Regression

28. ANOVA as a Special Case of Regression

ANOVA can be understood as a special case of regression with categorical independent variables.

In regression, categorical variables can be represented using dummy variables. A regression model with categorical predictors can reproduce ANOVA results.

Regression is more flexible because it can include:

  • categorical variables
  • interval-level variables
  • multiple controls
  • interactions
  • nonlinear terms
  • complex model structures

For this reason, many applied researchers use regression rather than ANOVA.

Still, ANOVA is conceptually useful because it teaches us how variation in an outcome can be partitioned into explained and unexplained components.

29. When Is ANOVA Useful?

ANOVA is useful when:

  • the dependent variable is interval-level
  • the independent variable or variables are categorical
  • we want to test whether group means differ
  • we have more than two group means
  • we want a broad test of whether categories explain variation in an outcome

Examples:

  • comparing test scores across schools
  • comparing outcomes across multiple treatment arms
  • comparing attitudes across party categories
  • comparing health outcomes across income groups
  • testing whether several group means are equal

ANOVA is less useful when:

  • we need to control for many variables
  • variables are continuous
  • the model requires more flexible specification
  • we care about estimating specific effect sizes
  • causal inference requires more advanced design

In those cases, regression is usually preferable.

Part VIII: Interpreting ANOVA Carefully

30. ANOVA Tells Us Whether Means Differ

ANOVA tells us whether at least one group mean differs from the others.

It does not automatically tell us:

  • which groups differ
  • how large the differences are
  • whether the differences are substantively important
  • whether the relationship is causal

If ANOVA rejects the null hypothesis, researchers may conduct follow-up comparisons to determine which group means differ. These are sometimes called post-hoc tests.

31. Statistical Versus Substantive Significance

As always, statistical significance is not the same as substantive importance.

With a very large sample, small differences in means may produce a statistically significant F statistic.

For example, regional differences in Obama thermometer scores may be statistically significant because the sample is large, even if the difference is only a few points on a 0-to-100 scale.

Researchers must inspect the group means and ask:

Are these differences meaningful in the real world?

ANOVA provides statistical evidence. Interpretation requires substantive judgment.

32. Causality

ANOVA does not establish causality by itself.

If the data come from a randomized experiment, group differences may support causal claims.

If the data are observational, group differences may reflect confounding, selection, or other alternative explanations.

For example, if democracies have higher life expectancy than non-democracies, the difference may reflect wealth, health systems, education, colonial history, conflict, geography, or other factors.

ANOVA can help describe and test associations. Causal inference requires research design and theory.

Key Terms

ANOVA
Analysis of variance; a method for comparing means across multiple groups.

One-way ANOVA
ANOVA with one categorical independent variable.

Two-way ANOVA
ANOVA with two categorical independent variables.

Dependent variable
The interval-level outcome variable whose mean is compared across groups.

Independent variable
A categorical variable used to define groups.

Group mean
The mean of the dependent variable within a category.

Grand mean
The overall mean of the dependent variable across all observations.

Between-group variation
Variation in group means around the grand mean.

Within-group variation
Variation of individual observations around their group means.

Between sum of squares
A measure of variation explained by group differences.

Within sum of squares
A measure of variation remaining within groups.

Mean square between
Between sum of squares divided by between-group degrees of freedom.

Mean square within
Within sum of squares divided by within-group degrees of freedom.

F statistic
The ratio of between-group variance to within-group variance.

F distribution
The probability distribution used to evaluate F statistics.

Numerator degrees of freedom
Degrees of freedom associated with between-group variation.

Denominator degrees of freedom
Degrees of freedom associated with within-group variation.

Residual variation
Variation not explained by the group categories.

R-squared
The proportion of total variation explained by the model.

Post-hoc comparisons
Follow-up tests used to determine which specific group means differ after ANOVA rejects the null hypothesis.

Chapter Summary

ANOVA is used to compare more than two means simultaneously. It tests whether all group means are equal or whether at least one differs.

The logic of ANOVA is based on partitioning variance. Total variation in an interval-level outcome is divided into between-group variation and within-group variation. If group means differ substantially relative to variation within groups, the F statistic becomes large.

The F statistic is:

[ F = ]

A large F statistic with a small p-value leads us to reject the null hypothesis that all group means are equal.

One-way ANOVA uses one categorical independent variable. Two-way ANOVA uses two categorical independent variables and allows us to test whether each variable explains variation in the outcome after controlling for the other.

ANOVA can be useful for comparing means across groups, but it has limits. It does not identify which groups differ unless additional comparisons are performed. It does not establish causality unless supported by an appropriate research design. And in many applied settings, regression provides a more flexible framework.

The core lesson is that ANOVA helps us evaluate whether categorical group membership explains variation in an interval-level outcome. It extends the logic of difference-of-means tests to situations with multiple groups.
