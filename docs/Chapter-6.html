Chapter 6: Sampling Distributions

Sampling distributions are one of the most important ideas in statistics.

They are also one of the easiest ideas to confuse with something else. A sample distribution is the distribution of values in the sample you actually collected. A sampling distribution is a probability distribution for a sample statistic, such as a sample mean or sample proportion, across repeated random samples.

That distinction matters.

In earlier chapters, we studied probability distributions. We learned that random variables have distributions, and that those distributions can be described by their centers, spreads, and shapes. We also studied the normal distribution and learned that approximately 68 percent of the area under a normal curve lies within one standard deviation of the mean, while approximately 95 percent lies within 1.96 standard deviations.

Now we apply those ideas to sample statistics.

A sample mean is random because it depends on which cases happen to be selected into the sample. A sample proportion is random for the same reason. If we drew a different sample, we would likely get a different mean or proportion. Therefore, sample statistics have probability distributions.

Those probability distributions are called sampling distributions.

Understanding sampling distributions allows us to answer one of the central questions of statistical inference:

How close is our sample statistic likely to be to the population parameter?

That question is the foundation for confidence intervals, hypothesis tests, standard errors, p-values, and much of the rest of statistical analysis.

1. From Probability Distributions to Sampling Distributions

A probability distribution describes the probabilities associated with the possible values of a random variable.

A sampling distribution is a special kind of probability distribution. It describes the probabilities associated with the possible values of a sample statistic.

Examples of sample statistics include:

  • a sample mean, ({X})
  • a sample proportion, ()
  • a sample standard deviation, (s)
  • a difference between two sample means
  • a regression coefficient

These statistics are random variables because they are calculated from random samples.

Once we have a sample, the statistic is fixed. If we calculate the mean of our sample, there is only one value it can take for that sample. But before we collect the sample, we do not know what the mean will be. Different random samples would produce different sample means.

A sampling distribution describes how those sample means, proportions, or other statistics would vary across repeated random samples.

The expected value of a fair die roll is:

Now imagine that everyone in a class rolls a die 200 times and calculates their own mean. Each person would get a different sample mean. Those means would vary because each set of 200 rolls is a different random sample from the same underlying probability distribution.

The sample mean is therefore a random variable.

Now imagine repeating this process not 40 or 50 times, but 1,000 times. We would have 1,000 sample means. If we graphed those means, we would see a distribution.

That distribution is the sampling distribution of the sample mean.

3. What a Sampling Distribution Shows

A sampling distribution tells us how a statistic behaves across repeated random samples.

For example, the sampling distribution of ({X}) tells us the probability that the sample mean falls in different ranges.

It answers questions like:

  • How likely is the sample mean to be close to the population mean?
  • How likely is it to be far away?
  • How much does the sample mean vary across repeated samples?
  • What is the typical size of random sampling error?

In practice, we usually do not observe the sampling distribution. We usually collect one sample and calculate one sample mean. We do not repeatedly collect thousands of samples.

But statistical theory tells us what the sampling distribution looks like under certain conditions. Simulation can help us see the idea, but theory is what lets us use it in real research.

4. Sample Distribution Versus Sampling Distribution

Because the terms sound similar, it is worth being very clear.

A sample distribution is the distribution of the actual data in one sample.

For example, if we survey 1,000 people and record their incomes, the sample distribution shows how those 1,000 incomes are distributed.

A sampling distribution is the distribution of a sample statistic across repeated samples.

For example, if we repeatedly survey 1,000 people, calculate the mean income in each sample, and then graph all those sample means, that graph is the sampling distribution of the sample mean.

The sample distribution is observed.
The sampling distribution is theoretical.

The sample distribution describes values of individual observations.
The sampling distribution describes values of statistics.

This distinction is central.

5. Why Sampling Distributions Matter

Sampling distributions matter because they allow us to estimate uncertainty.

Suppose we have a sample mean and want to use it to estimate the population mean. We know the sample mean is probably not exactly equal to the population mean. It is affected by random sampling error.

The key question is:

How much random sampling error should we expect?

Sampling distributions allow us to answer that.

If the sampling distribution is narrow, sample means tend to fall close to the population mean. Our estimate is relatively precise.

If the sampling distribution is wide, sample means vary more across samples. Our estimate is less precise.

This is why sampling distributions are the foundation for confidence intervals. A confidence interval uses information about the sampling distribution to create a range of plausible values for the population parameter.

Sampling distributions are also the foundation for hypothesis testing. A hypothesis test asks whether an observed sample statistic would be likely or unlikely if a particular hypothesis about the population were true.

7. The Shape of the Sampling Distribution

The first major insight of the Central Limit Theorem is that the sampling distribution of the sample mean is approximately normal under the right conditions.

This is powerful because the population distribution itself does not have to be normal.

The original population might be:

  • uniform
  • skewed
  • bimodal
  • irregular
  • lumpy, odd, or otherwise statistically unglamorous

Even so, if the sample size is large enough, the distribution of sample means will be approximately normal.

This is one reason the normal distribution is so important. It appears not only when individual variables are normally distributed, but also when we look at averages from random samples.

The sample mean does not systematically overestimate or underestimate the population mean. It may be wrong in any single sample, but it is right on average.

10. Conditions for the Central Limit Theorem

The Central Limit Theorem does not apply automatically in every possible situation. Several conditions matter.

First, the sample should be randomly drawn.

Second, the observations should be independent, or at least close enough to independent for the methods to work.

Third, the sample size must be large enough.

A common rule of thumb is:

[ n ]

If the sample size is at least 30, the sampling distribution of the sample mean is usually approximately normal, even if the population distribution is not normal.

This is a rule of thumb, not a magic boundary. A sample size of 29 is not suddenly terrible while 30 is perfect. The adequacy of the approximation depends on the shape of the population distribution.

If the population is highly skewed or has extreme outliers, we may need a larger sample.

If the population is already approximately normal, even a smaller sample may be sufficient.

11. When the Population Is Normal

If the population distribution is normal, then the sampling distribution of the sample mean is normal regardless of sample size.

That means even small samples can produce normally distributed sample means when the original population is normal.

Why?

Because a normal population is symmetric. Extreme values on one side are balanced by extreme values on the other side. The sample mean does not inherit skewness because there is no skewness to inherit.

This differs from a skewed population. If the population is strongly skewed and the sample size is small, an extreme observation can pull the sample mean strongly in one direction. In that case, the sampling distribution may also be skewed.

12. When the Population Is Skewed

Suppose the population distribution is positively skewed, as income often is.

Most observations are clustered toward lower and middle values, but a small number of observations extend far into the upper tail.

If we draw very small samples, such as samples of size 5, some samples may include one of those extreme high values. When that happens, the sample mean is pulled upward. Other samples will not include such values and will have lower means.

As a result, the sampling distribution of the mean may also be skewed.

But as sample size increases, the influence of any one extreme value decreases. Larger samples better represent the full population distribution. The skewness begins to wash out, and the sampling distribution becomes more normal.

This is the Central Limit Theorem at work.

13. Why Sample Size Matters

Sample size matters for two reasons.

First, larger samples make the sampling distribution more normal.

Second, larger samples make the sampling distribution narrower.

A narrower sampling distribution means sample means are more tightly clustered around the population mean. That means less random sampling error.

This is why larger samples produce more precise estimates.

25. Precision and Confidence

A wider interval captures more possible sample means. That makes it more reliable in the sense that it covers a larger portion of the sampling distribution.

But it is less precise because it spans a wider range of values.

This creates a tradeoff:

  • A 68 percent range is narrower but less confident.
  • A 95 percent range is wider but more confident.
  • A 99 percent range would be wider still.

Greater confidence requires a wider interval.

This will become central when we study confidence intervals.

Part VI: The Big Picture

29. The Central Insight

The central insight of this chapter is:

Sample statistics vary across repeated samples, but they vary in predictable ways.

A sample mean is usually not exactly equal to the population mean. A sample proportion is usually not exactly equal to the population proportion. But probability theory tells us how far away those sample statistics are likely to be.

The Central Limit Theorem tells us that the sampling distribution of the sample mean is approximately normal under broad conditions.

30. Why This Matters for Inference

Statistical inference is about using sample data to make claims about populations.

Sampling distributions make inference possible.

They allow us to say things like:

  • A sample mean this far from the hypothesized population mean would be very unlikely if the hypothesis were true.
  • A plausible range for the population mean is this interval around the sample mean.
  • Larger samples produce smaller standard errors and therefore more precise estimates.
  • If the sample statistic is many standard errors away from a hypothesized value, that hypothesis may not be plausible.

Everything that follows—confidence intervals, significance tests, p-values, and regression inference—depends on this logic.

Chapter Summary

A sampling distribution is the probability distribution of a sample statistic across repeated random samples. It is not the same as a sample distribution, which describes the observed values in one sample.

Sample statistics are random variables because they depend on random samples. Different samples produce different sample means, proportions, and standard deviations. Sampling distributions describe how those statistics vary.

The Central Limit Theorem tells us that the sampling distribution of the sample mean is approximately normal under broad conditions. It is centered at the true population mean, and its standard deviation is the standard error:

[ SE_{{X}} = ]

The standard error tells us the typical amount of random sampling error. It decreases as sample size increases and increases as population variability increases.

Because the sampling distribution is approximately normal, we can use normal distribution logic to say that about 68 percent of sample means fall within one standard error of the population mean and about 95 percent fall within 1.96 standard errors.

In real research, we usually do not know the population standard deviation, so we estimate the standard error using the sample standard deviation. This leads us to the t-distribution, which we use when accounting for the extra uncertainty introduced by estimation.

Sampling distributions are the foundation of statistical inference. They allow us to move from sample data to population claims while quantifying uncertainty.
