Chapter 7: Statistical Inference

We now shift from theoretical foundations to application.

So far, we have built the machinery of statistical inference. We have learned about probability, probability distributions, the normal distribution, the binomial distribution, sampling distributions, the Central Limit Theorem, and standard errors. Those ideas may have seemed abstract at times, but they now begin to do real work.

The central problem is this:

We have sample data, but we want to say something about a population.

A sample statistic, such as a sample mean or sample proportion, is our best estimate of the corresponding population parameter. But we know that the sample statistic is probably not exactly correct. It is affected by random sampling error.

Statistical inference gives us tools for communicating how precise we think our estimate is.

The first major tool is the confidence interval.

A confidence interval uses sample data and probability theory to construct a range around a point estimate. This range communicates the precision of the estimate. It does not tell us exactly where the population parameter is. Rather, it tells us what our statistical procedure is capable of capturing across repeated samples.

This chapter introduces statistical inference through confidence intervals. We begin with preliminaries, then discuss the logic of confidence intervals, including confidence levels, margins of error, standard errors, and critical values. We then construct confidence intervals for means and proportions.

1. The Big Idea of Statistical Inference

Statistical inference is the process of using sample data to make claims about a population.

We usually cannot observe the full population. Instead, we collect a sample and calculate sample statistics.

For example:

  • We use a sample mean ({Y}) to estimate a population mean ().
  • We use a sample proportion () to estimate a population proportion ().
  • We use a sample standard deviation (s) to estimate a population standard deviation ().

These sample statistics are point estimates.

A point estimate is a single-number estimate of a population parameter.

For example, suppose the Current Population Survey estimates that the unemployment rate in the United States is 4.3 percent. That 4.3 percent is a point estimate. It is based on sample data, not a complete count of every person in the country.

Because it comes from a sample, it is probably not exactly equal to the true unemployment rate. It is an estimate.

The natural question is:

How precise is that estimate?

That is what confidence intervals help us communicate.

[ 47 - 2 = 45 ]

[ 47 + 2 = 49 ]

The margin of error communicates the precision of the estimate.

A smaller margin of error means the estimate is more precise.
A larger margin of error means the estimate is less precise.

Part II: Confidence Intervals

7. What Is a Confidence Interval?

A confidence interval is a numerical interval constructed around a point estimate using a procedure that captures the true population parameter a specified percentage of the time across repeated random samples.

That definition is careful for a reason.

A 95 percent confidence interval does not mean:

There is a 95 percent chance that the true population parameter is inside this specific interval.

That is the tempting interpretation, but it is not technically correct.

Instead, a 95 percent confidence interval means:

If we repeatedly drew random samples and constructed confidence intervals using this same procedure, 95 percent of those intervals would contain the true population parameter.

The confidence level describes the long-run success rate of the procedure.

Part III: Interpreting Confidence Intervals Correctly

12. What Confidence Intervals Do Not Mean

A confidence interval is often misinterpreted.

Suppose we calculate a 95 percent confidence interval for a mean and get:

[ 9.752 10.248 ]

It is tempting to say:

There is a 95 percent chance that the true population mean lies between 9.752 and 10.248.

But that is not technically correct.

The true population mean is fixed. It is a number. We do not know it, but it is not random.

The interval is random before we collect the sample because the sample is random. Once we collect the sample and calculate the interval, the interval is fixed too.

At that point, the true mean either is inside the interval or it is not.

So the probability is not 0.95 for this specific interval. It is either 1 or 0. We just do not know which.

13. The Ring Toss Analogy

A useful analogy is a ring toss.

Imagine the true population mean is a stake in the ground. We cannot see the stake. We are blindfolded.

Our statistical procedure is like tossing a ring toward the stake.

A 95 percent confidence procedure means that, in the long run, 95 percent of the rings we toss will land around the stake.

But once a ring has landed, it either surrounds the stake or it does not. It no longer makes sense to say there is a 95 percent chance that this particular ring surrounds the stake.

The probability describes the procedure before the toss, not the ring after it lands.

The same is true for confidence intervals.

Before collecting data, there is a 95 percent chance that the interval we will construct will contain the true parameter.

After collecting data, the interval either contains the true parameter or it does not.

14. Correct Interpretations

A good interpretation of a 95 percent confidence interval is:

Across repeated random samples, 95 percent of confidence intervals constructed using this procedure would contain the true population mean.

Or:

We used a procedure that, in repeated sampling, produces intervals containing the true population mean 95 percent of the time.

Then, if useful, we can state the interval:

In this case, the interval ranges from 9.752 to 10.248.

What we should avoid is language like:

There is a 95 percent probability that the true mean is between 9.752 and 10.248.

or:

The true mean falls between 9.752 and 10.248 with probability 0.95.

Those statements incorrectly treat the population parameter as random after the interval has already been constructed.

17. Why the t-Distribution?

The t-distribution looks similar to the normal distribution, but it has more area in the tails.

That extra tail area reflects additional uncertainty from estimating () with (s).

When sample size is small, (s) may be a noisy estimate of (). The t-distribution accounts for that by producing wider intervals.

As sample size increases, the t-distribution becomes nearly identical to the standard normal distribution.

For large samples, using (t) or (z) makes almost no difference.

For small samples, using (t) matters.

18. Degrees of Freedom

The t-distribution depends on degrees of freedom.

For a one-sample confidence interval for a mean, the degrees of freedom are:

[ df = n - 1 ]

The smaller the degrees of freedom, the heavier the tails of the t-distribution.

As degrees of freedom increase, the t-distribution approaches the standard normal distribution.

In practice:

  • With small samples, use the t-distribution.
  • With large samples, the t-distribution and normal distribution are nearly the same.
  • For means, using the t-distribution is generally safe.

19. Conditions for a Confidence Interval for a Mean

To construct a confidence interval for a mean using the usual t procedure, we need appropriate conditions.

The sample should be random.

The observations should be independent or approximately independent.

The sampling distribution of the mean should be approximately normal.

That last condition is satisfied if either:

  1. The population distribution is approximately normal, or
  2. The sample size is large enough, usually (n ).

If the sample size is less than 30 and the population distribution is strongly skewed or otherwise non-normal, the usual t procedure may not be appropriate.

The t-distribution adjusts for estimating (). It does not fix a non-normal sampling distribution caused by a small sample from a non-normal population.

That distinction is important.

24. Why We Use z for Proportions

For proportions, we do not use the t-distribution.

This is important.

For means, we use the t-distribution because we estimate the population standard deviation () with the sample standard deviation (s).

For proportions, the relevant distribution is fundamentally connected to the binomial distribution. The binomial distribution gives the exact probabilities of different numbers of successes in a fixed number of trials.

When sample size is large enough, the binomial distribution can be approximated by the normal distribution. Then we use the z-distribution.

So for proportions:

  • Small samples: use the binomial distribution.
  • Large samples: use the normal approximation with z.
  • Do not use the t-distribution for proportions.

Part VII: The Logic of Precision

31. What Makes a Confidence Interval Wider?

A confidence interval becomes wider when:

  1. The confidence level increases.
  2. The standard error increases.
  3. The sample size decreases.
  4. The sample or population is more variable.

For means, the standard error is:

[ ]

So a larger (s) means a wider interval.

A larger (n) means a narrower interval.

For proportions, the standard error is:

[ ]

Again, a larger (n) produces a narrower interval.

The confidence level also matters. A 99 percent confidence interval is wider than a 95 percent confidence interval because it uses a larger critical value.

32. What Makes a Confidence Interval Narrower?

A confidence interval becomes narrower when:

  1. The confidence level decreases.
  2. The standard error decreases.
  3. The sample size increases.
  4. The data are less variable.

Larger samples give more precise estimates because they reduce standard error.

This is one of the most important practical lessons in statistics:

Precision improves with sample size, but at a diminishing rate because standard error decreases with (), not (n).

For example, quadrupling the sample size cuts the standard error in half.

That is useful, but it also means precision becomes increasingly expensive to improve.

Key Terms

Statistical inference
The process of using sample data to make claims about a population.

Point estimate
A single sample statistic used to estimate a population parameter.

Estimator
A statistic used to estimate a population parameter.

Interval estimate
A range of values used to communicate uncertainty around a point estimate.

Normal approximation
Using the normal distribution to approximate another distribution, such as the binomial, when sample size is large enough.

Binomial distribution
The exact distribution for the number of successes in a fixed number of independent trials with two possible outcomes.

Chapter Summary

Statistical inference allows us to use sample data to make claims about populations while acknowledging uncertainty.

A point estimate gives a single best estimate of a population parameter, such as a mean or proportion. But because sample statistics vary from sample to sample, point estimates are uncertain.

A confidence interval communicates the precision of a point estimate. It has the general form:

The confidence level describes the long-run success rate of the procedure. A 95 percent confidence interval procedure produces intervals that contain the true population parameter 95 percent of the time across repeated random samples.

It is important not to say that there is a 95 percent chance that the true parameter lies inside a specific interval. Once the interval is calculated, the parameter either is inside it or it is not. The probability refers to the procedure, not the fixed interval.

For means, we usually construct confidence intervals using the t-distribution:

[ {Y} t^*() ]

For proportions, we usually use the z-distribution when the sample size is large enough:

[ z^* ]

For small-sample proportions, the binomial distribution is more appropriate.

The central lesson is that confidence intervals quantify uncertainty. They do not eliminate uncertainty, but they give us a disciplined way to communicate how precise our estimates are.
